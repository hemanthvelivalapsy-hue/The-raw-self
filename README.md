THE RAW SELF

By
HEMANTH VELIVALA
INTRODUCTION
This book consists of independent theories primarily rooted in metacognition and developed through meticulous introspection of real-life events. To maintain the purity and internal consistency of the ideas explored here, I have intentionally avoided grounding this work in established psychological or philosophical schools of thought. These conclusions are the product of independent logic; any resemblance to external theories is unintentional and purely coincidental.
By the end of the book, you should have a clear understanding of the “raw self” and how your brain tricks you into avoiding it.
UNDERSTANDING THE "RAW SELF"
What is the raw self?
It is important to understand that the raw self isn't an animal instinct or an "alpha" version of yourself; it is simply the state where you can essentially see through yourself. For instance, let's assume you penetrate through all your coping mechanisms, survival strategies, and ego. This is the state where you can see the real you: the raw self.
On first thought, it sounds so simple! The concept is pretty straightforward, but finding the key to unlocking that state is like navigating a "Quasicrystal Maze." To get a better understanding of how our mind tricks us into avoiding the raw self, let's analyze the situation of John.
John is a male in his early 20s who is going through a very rough phase and has been diagnosed with depression. He coincidentally meets Beth, who is in a very similar situation. They start talking and quickly fall in love. Now, I want you to pause and think of the first sentence John might say to Beth when he proposes to her. If you guessed, “Fate brought us together; we were destined to be partners,” you were spot on!
ANALYZING THE "COINCIDENCE"
The core of the situation is that John is in a tough phase. The more time he spends alone, the more he has to face his thoughts. Over time, these thoughts grow in magnitude and penetrate his coping mechanisms, exposing his raw self.
Think of yourself as a finger! The only catch is that the outer layer of your skin cannot be regenerated. Let’s assume all of your coping mechanisms are your outer skin and the raw self is the inside. When your skin is peeled off or exposed, it stings. Similarly, when your coping mechanisms are penetrated, your brain perceives it as a threat and does its maximum to create an alternative.
Unlike your real skin, which regenerates easily, coping mechanisms cannot be "repaired" once exposed. Therefore, your brain finds alternatives, regardless of the price it pays. The price paid could be anything from drugs to creating an entirely alternate reality.
A clear example of this is the case study of Chuck McGill from Better Call Saul. Though the character is fictional, he helps us understand the concept. When Chuck McGill realizes that his brother whom he considered incapable manages to get a law degree, Chuck is forced to see him as a peer. His brain couldn't take it. One of his vital coping mechanisms was the belief that the law is only for "good" people. Because Jimmy clearly didn't fit that mold and yet became a lawyer, Chuck’s ego was exposed. He developed electromagnetic sensitivity, which was simply an alternative reality his brain created when it was exposed to its raw self.
CIRCLING BACK TO JOHN
As we have now established the fact that the more time he spends alone, the more exposed he is, his brain starts to look for alternatives:
1. Do drugs or develop a similar addiction to numb himself.
2. Go to therapy and talk through it.
3. Find an alternative solution other than 1 and 2.
Option 1, though the easiest of all, comes with the most guilt. No matter how much blame John tries to externalize on the situation, a little bit of it always stays on "him." This option is usually taken by people who are ready to take the blame for the temporary numbing. This usually ends up horribly wrong since, when the temporary numbing wears off, John is exposed to his raw self but now with added guilt that it was his decision to take drugs.
Option 2 sounds very simple, but talking with someone about your issues means you not only expose yourself to the raw self, but it happens in front of someone. This means you are doubly vulnerable than usual and, subconsciously, you are constantly looking for their validation, since the power of the dynamic is completely in their hands. As this is high risk and our brain is naturally wired for survival, this usually doesn’t happen.
Option 3: John somehow magically finds someone, maybe a partner. It’s important to note that John isn’t consciously taking any of the decisions; it’s his subconscious that's driving him. What option 3 does is it gives him the temporary numbing a similar effect to option 1, i.e., physical intimacy but minus the guilt, since he isn’t consciously doing it this time.
UNDERSTANDING THE MECHANISM
By now, you must have developed the understanding that our brain avoids the raw self when it’s cornered. But what if I told you it does the same not only to survive, but to hunt?
Since we have understood the first extreme, let’s delve into the second. To get a better visualization, let’s take a simple example from our society. People are often separated by religion, skin color, or gender. There is constant hatred, but why? Is it politics, or something else entirely?
I was forced to think about this when my little sister asked why there is hate among communities for no “apparent” reason. Instead of using fancy psychological terms, let’s understand it the real way.
Now, let’s say a king tells his people, “We’ve killed about 10,000 people in the war.” How would those people feel? Guilty? Surely. Now, let’s assume the same king says, “We’ve killed about 10,000 people in the war to spread our God's words.”
How does this make the people feel now? Why is there a sense of pride? Some feel a sense of pride and others feel relieved, but they certainly don't feel guilt. Why? Because the blame is being completely externalized in this scenario, in the form of God.
This is the same situation we see in the world right now. You see people from both the left and the right justify wars by claiming they are “making our country great for future generations.” Instead of God, the blame is externalized onto non-existing people. Since this provides a sense of sacrifice, the sense of pride is doubled compared to the previous scenario.
UNDERSTANDING THE MECHANISM: CASE II
Since we’ve seen the two extremes, let’s take the middle ground. How does our mind steer us away from the raw self in our daily lives? At this level, we should drop the idea that our brain is being "forced" into a life-or-death decision.
Consider Johan. He’s in his mid-20s and, like all of us, he’s very active on social media. He frequently uploads stories and posts. Now, Johan’s mom, Maria, is told about her son’s frequent online activity by a "secret someone." She tells her son to get off social media for a month. Now, Johan is smart. He figures he could use a "burner" account for the time being; once the month passes, he can easily go back to his main account. In this scenario, Johan still has access to the app, but not to his established identity.
Note what happened here: Johan didn’t lose access to the app, but he lost access to his identity. He lost the validation and approval of the people who visit his profile. Since that validation is snatched away, he loses his sense of power. This exposes his raw self. This only lasts until he gets frustrated and the layers slowly start to leak, showing the raw self underneath.
THE VALIDATION COMPETITION: VELIVALA’S THEORY
This is the section where we go deeper into the mechanism using our established knowledge. To get a grasp on this, let’s go back to John. As we know, John and Beth are in a relationship, but let’s change the dynamics slightly.
Beth has been struggling mentally. Instead of confronting her raw self, she chooses an alternate path: dating people for the "spike" that numbs her pain. This is identical to the "Option 1" (Numbing) we discussed earlier. She meets a guy named Peter. She "falls in love" quickly which isn't unusual, as she is using the relationship as an escape.
But something happens: Peter actually tries to help her. He acknowledges her past and tries to guide her through it. This forces Beth to face her raw self while simultaneously becoming addicted to Peter’s validation. Since Peter is the one "helping," the power dynamic shifts completely in his favor. Once the honeymoon phase ends and things smooth out, the constant "nod" of validation from Peter stops. Beth becomes desperate. Her solution? To continue the cycle. She "magically" meets John and cheats on Peter to be with him.
John is in a similar phase; he was recently rejected by a crush and is looking for a way to avoid the sting. Now he is with Beth who is famous, beautiful, and "out of his league." The honeymoon phase is fantastic. Because Beth carries the guilt of cheating on Peter, she doubles her efforts with John to drown out that guilt.
This is where the Validation Competition begins.
Since John sees someone "out of his league" putting in double the effort, he pushes himself to match her. They both compete, masking it as "love," but in reality, they are feeding off each other like parasites. This has three major consequences:
• A) The Intensity Peak: The competition becomes so intense that the dopamine spike becomes the "new normal." Soon, the spike vanishes. To find that high again, they make rash, drastic decisions moving in too fast, committing too early, or eloping just to feel a new surge of validation.
• B) The One-Sided Win: In this game, winning is worse than losing. Love is rarely equal. If Beth "wins" by out-competing John, John reaches a state where he receives validation without doing any work. He becomes a parasite feeding off Beth's desperation. Eventually, John either breaks up with her because his "raw self" is now fully bandaged and he doesn't "need" her anymore or he becomes distant and cold.
• C) The Guilt Paradox: Let’s assume a "positive" outcome where John genuinely falls in love with Beth. Every kind word John says and every effort he makes actually makes Beth feel worse. Why? Because it triggers her guilt toward Peter. By being "good" to her, John indirectly reminds her that she is a "jerk" for how she left Peter. To escape this new sting, she either retreats or enters a cycle of double-dating, which never ends well.
Who is to blame here? John or Beth? The answer is no one. In the mechanism of the raw self, the damage is rarely linear.
THE FAMILY INHERITANCE: CASE IV
This section discusses what builds the raw self and the role parental influence plays. We will explore what makes Beth avoid her raw self, but to understand her upbringing, let’s look at someone more famous and "deadly": Dexter Morgan.
What makes Dexter a serial killer? Was it Harry’s upbringing? The answer is a certain yes. But then, why did Brian Moser (Dexter's brother) become one too, without Harry’s manipulation? It’s not about what they could become; it’s about what could’ve been prevented. Harry was a great cop, but he saw "bad" people escape the law. His raw self couldn’t handle the fact that bad people got away. Instead of acknowledging this, he built a layer over it by forcing his ideologies onto Dexter. By teaching him that killing "bad" people is justice, Harry hid from his own raw self. In reality, this is just another form of externalizing blame to avoid guilt similar to the King example used earlier, but using "Justice" as the external god.
Now, back to Beth. Many people from Asia experience "tough parenting," which has a genuine impact on how children run from their raw self. Kim, Beth’s mom, was known for being strict. She forbade parties, discouraged relationships, and claimed that "lust" was the only reason to talk to boys. Kim didn't realize she was building a cage. By forcing her ideologies, she created two impacts:
• A) Subconscious Validation Craving: Because Beth was restricted, she subconsciously craves the validation she never received from her mother. She searches for this in Peter or John, which inevitably turns her relationships into toxic dynamics.
• B) The Fear of the Wild: By instilling the fear that all relationships are just "lust," Kim ensured that when Beth finally had the freedom to date, she was too guarded or highly worried. It is like trapping a bird in a cage for five years and then asking it to fly in the wild.
In both cases Beth and Dexter the effect is a cry for help. Beth searches for what she lacked by dating too many people, trying to make a point but getting caught in a parasitic loop. Dexter, despite being a seasoned killer, hides his blood slides in an air conditioner. Why? Because subconsciously, he knows the "layer" is too much for him. He wants out.
CASE V: THE MAGNETISM: Hemanth’s "Void-Link"
This section is intended to categorize the examples we used earlier specifically the dynamic between Beth and John discussed previously. By revisiting them here, we can understand the fundamental "Why" behind their attraction without being repetitive. We are looking at why specific raw selves gravitate toward each other.
Since we covered Dexter recently, he is the best person to help us understand this mechanic. Dexter’s famous line when he meets his partner is: “She’s perfect. She’s as damaged as I am, in her own way.”
What does he mean by that? In the earlier pages, we saw how Beth and John used each other to fill gaps in a validation battle. That wasn't a random pairing; it was The Magnetism. Now, let’s apply this to a different area: Debra Morgan.
Why does she consistently choose older, unavailable partners? We know that Harry often ignored her or didn’t give her the appreciation he gave to Dexter. When Harry passed away, Deb didn’t get the closure she needed. When humans don't get closure, they get desperate. She started searching for that missing validation in unavailable, distant partners just to run from her Raw Self.
The Void of the Layer:
Deb didn't just dedicate her personal life to this; she dedicated her work life to becoming a Lieutenant just to make her dead father proud. But why didn't she get closure? Because her achievement was just a layer on the Raw Self.
The tragedy is this: the moment you reach a goal set as a layer, you find yourself in a Void. Because it was a "never-ending" answer, reaching it leaves you lost. The need for a new, deeper layer becomes even more desperate.
Who is to blame here? Is it Deb? Or is it Harry, based on our Family Inheritance theory?
SECTION VI: THE DECOMPOSITION
This section will briefly explain how to deconstruct our layers to reach our raw selves.
The key is being gentle and acknowledging our mistakes. Why gentle? Because the slower you are, the more dots you connect; the more dots you connect, the closer you are to your raw self.
The next very important step involves stopping the externalization of blame and taking accountability. This ensures any mistakes you’ve made in the past aren’t repeated.
A very normal response would be a phenomenon of what they call PTG (Post-Traumatic Growth). You’ll naturally realize your guilt will be converted into empathy. This empathy is often linear, meaning helping people or creatures who aren’t malicious. How do you know they don’t possess intent? It’s because when you are at the raw self, you cannot only see yourself, but see others at their raw state as well.
My opinion is that being human, and not at our raw state, means we easily externalize and run away from the blame. As humans, we have to stand up against fake agendas. Dopamine can be achieved in many other ways, such as helping others or genuinely focusing on yourself. The easier ways or shortcuts often lead to damaging others and ourselves subconsciously more than we realize.
